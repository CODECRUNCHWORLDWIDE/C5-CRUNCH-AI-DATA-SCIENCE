# Week 3 — Homework

Six problems, about six hours total. Commit each in your Week 3 repo under a `homework/` directory. Each problem expects either a small `.py` file or a short Markdown write-up; specifics below.

---

## Problem 1 — The publication checklist on your Week 2 chart (45 min)

Take the one chart from your Week 2 EDA mini-project (you made at least one in `pandas` `.plot` or matplotlib for the three-question section). Save it as `before.png` in `homework/01-before-after/`.

Now redo the same chart against the **publication checklist** from Lecture 3, Section 10. Save the new version as `after.png` in the same directory. Write `homework/01-before-after/notes.md` (≈200 words) listing every checklist item you changed and why. The list will be long — that is the point.

The hand-off rule: a reviewer who has not read your notebook should be able to look at `after.png` for five seconds and tell you what the chart claims.

**Acceptance.**

- `before.png` and `after.png` both committed.
- `notes.md` enumerates at least eight checklist items, each with a one-sentence justification.
- The "after" chart uses the OO API end-to-end (no `plt.title(...)`).

## Problem 2 — Predict-and-check on chart-type choice (30 min)

For each of these tasks, predict on paper:

1. Which library you would reach for (matplotlib / seaborn / plotly).
2. Which chart type.
3. Why, in one sentence.

Tasks:

1. EDA: "Show me the distribution of `trip_distance` for each of the four NYC boroughs."
2. Web dashboard: "Let our analysts hover over each country to see exact GDP per capita and life expectancy."
3. Slide deck: "Which five of our 50 product SKUs drove most of last quarter's revenue?"
4. Notebook for the team: "Is `body_mass_g` correlated with `flipper_length_mm` in the penguin data, and does the relationship differ by species?"
5. Newsroom story page: "How has the share of US electricity generated by solar changed since 2010?"
6. README of your portfolio repo: "Total trips per month in 2025."

Write your six predictions in `homework/02-chart-picker.md`. Then run the picker flowchart from Lecture 2, Section 10 and confirm or revise each. The point is to make the picker reflexive.

## Problem 3 — A "lies" tour (1 hour)

Find three real charts in the wild that exhibit at least one of the four common lies (truncated bar y-axis, dual-axis correlation theater, rainbow on ordinal, 3-D anything). Sources:

- Any corporate annual report (heavy 3-D pie charts, historically).
- A vendor's marketing site for a "data visualization" product.
- Reddit's [r/dataisugly](https://reddit.com/r/dataisugly) — a curated stream of bad charts.
- Local newspaper websites — political coverage often features truncated y-axes.

For each chart, in `homework/03-lies-tour.md`:

1. A screenshot or a stable link.
2. The name of the lie (one of the four).
3. A two-sentence diagnosis.
4. A one-paragraph honest replacement: what chart type, what encoding, what would you cut.

You are looking for three different lies if possible. If you find three of the same lie, that is fine — name what the most common lie is in your sample.

## Problem 4 — Color, applied (45 min)

Pick one of the following datasets:

- `seaborn.load_dataset("penguins")` — categorical species, continuous measurements.
- The Our World in Data CO2 dataset (`co2.csv` from <https://github.com/owid/co2-data>) — country, year, emissions; sequential magnitude.
- Your Week 2 cleaned DataFrame.

In `homework/04-color.py`:

1. Produce **three versions** of the same chart, varying only the color choice:
   - Version A: a categorical palette (`tab10` or similar) on a sequential magnitude. Save as `04-color-wrong.png`.
   - Version B: a sequential palette (`viridis`) on the same data. Save as `04-color-right.png`.
   - Version C: a diverging palette (`RdBu`) on the same data, centered on the mean. Save as `04-color-diverging.png`.
2. In `homework/04-color.md`, write 150 words on which version reads best for which question. Be specific: "Version B (`viridis`) makes the ordered magnitude visible at a glance because perceived lightness is uniform, whereas Version A (`tab10`) implies the categories are unordered."

The point is to feel the difference in your fingers. Three charts; one dataset; three color decisions.

## Problem 5 — Annotate a chart (45 min)

Take Exercise 3's time-series chart (or the chart from Problem 1) and add **two** annotations that move the chart from "data" to "story":

1. An annotation pointing at the largest outlier or the most informative inflection. Use `ax.annotate(..., arrowprops=dict(arrowstyle="->", ...))`.
2. A horizontal `ax.axhline` (or a vertical `ax.axvline`) at a reference value — the long-run mean, a forecast value, zero — with a small text label.

Save the result as `homework/05-annotated.png`. In `homework/05-annotated.md`, write 100 words on what the annotations add. The "before" version (the unannotated chart from Exercise 3 or Problem 1) is fine to reference; you do not need to re-commit it.

The exercise is about realizing that an unannotated chart is a draft, and an annotated chart is a story.

Write `homework/06-reflection.md` (250–400 words) answering:
